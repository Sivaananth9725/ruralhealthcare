PEDIATRIC EMERGENCY PROTOCOLS
=============================

SECTION 1: PEDIATRIC ASSESSMENT
==============================

Age-Based Vital Signs (Normal Ranges):

Newborn (0-3 months):
- Heart rate: 100-160 bpm
- Respiratory rate: 40-60 breaths/min
- Blood pressure: 50-70/25-45 mmHg
- Temperature: 36.5-37.5°C

Infant (3-12 months):
- Heart rate: 100-160 bpm
- Respiratory rate: 30-40 breaths/min
- Blood pressure: 80-100/50-60 mmHg
- Temperature: 36.5-37.5°C

Toddler (1-3 years):
- Heart rate: 90-150 bpm
- Respiratory rate: 25-35 breaths/min
- Blood pressure: 90-105/55-70 mmHg
- Temperature: 36.5-37.5°C

Preschool (3-6 years):
- Heart rate: 80-120 bpm
- Respiratory rate: 22-30 breaths/min
- Blood pressure: 95-110/60-75 mmHg
- Temperature: 36.5-37.5°C

School Age (6-12 years):
- Heart rate: 70-110 bpm
- Respiratory rate: 18-26 breaths/min
- Blood pressure: 105-135/70-85 mmHg
- Temperature: 36.5-37.5°C

Adolescent (12-18 years):
- Heart rate: 60-100 bpm
- Respiratory rate: 12-20 breaths/min
- Blood pressure: 110-140/70-90 mmHg
- Temperature: 36.5-37.5°C

Pediatric ABCDE Approach:
A - Appearance (alertness, activity, interaction)
B - Breathing (work of breathing, air exchange)
C - Circulation (heart rate, skin perfusion, blood pressure)
D - Disability (neurological status, glucose)
E - Exposure (complete examination, maintain temperature)

SECTION 2: RESPIRATORY DISTRESS IN CHILDREN
=============================================

Signs of Respiratory Distress:
- Fast breathing (tachypnea)
- Chest indrawing (lower chest, upper abdomen drawn in)
- Nasal flaring
- Grunting (expiratory sound)
- Use of neck muscles
- Stridor (musical breathing sound)
- Wheezing (whistling sound)
- Difficulty speaking/crying
- Lethargy or agitation
- Cyanosis (blue color)

Respiratory Rate Thresholds for Tachypnea:
< 2 months: ≥ 50 breaths/min
2-11 months: ≥ 40 breaths/min
12-59 months: ≥ 40 breaths/min
≥ 5 years: ≥ 30 breaths/min

Causes:
- Pneumonia
- Bronchiolitis
- Asthma
- Croup
- Whooping cough
- Foreign body aspiration
- Sepsis
- Heart disease
- Anemia
- Metabolic acidosis

URGENT REFERRAL (HIGH) Required For:
- Severe chest indrawing
- Stridor at rest
- Grunting
- Cyanosis
- Severe lethargy
- Inability to drink

SECTION 3: SEVERE DIARRHEA AND DEHYDRATION
=========================================

Dehydration Classification:

MILD-MODERATE (3-7% fluid loss):
- Alert, normal behavior
- Normal eyes
- Drinks normally
- Skin pinch goes back quickly (< 2 seconds)
- Urine output: decreased
- Mucous membranes: slightly dry

SEVERE (≥ 10% fluid loss):
- Lethargy or restlessness
- Sunken eyes
- Drinks poorly or unable to drink
- Skin pinch goes back slowly (> 2 seconds)
- Urine: minimal or absent
- Mucous membranes: very dry
- May have weak pulse
- May be cold/clammy
- May have shock

REFER URGENTLY (HIGH) For:
- Severe dehydration signs
- Unable to drink
- Persistent vomiting
- Bloody diarrhea
- Severe malnutrition
- Age < 6 months
- Signs of shock
- Severe abdominal pain

Management:
- ORS 50-100 mL/kg over 4 hours
- After each stool: 10 mL/kg ORS
- Continue breastfeeding
- Reassess after 4 hours
- If no improvement: refer
- If improved: maintenance + replacement

SECTION 4: FEVER IN YOUNG CHILDREN
=================================

Temperature Recording:
- Rectal: most accurate in infants
- Axillary: convenient, slightly lower
- Oral: use after 5 years
- Tympanic: variable accuracy

Assessment:
- Duration and pattern
- Associated symptoms
- Vaccination status
- Risk factors for serious infection
- Appearance and behavior

Red Flags in Febrile Children (HIGH):
- Age < 3 months
- Very high fever (> 40°C)
- Fever with petechial rash (non-blanching)
- Stiff neck
- Bulging fontanelle (in infants)
- Altered consciousness
- Severe lethargy
- Convulsions
- Severe respiratory distress
- Unable to drink

REFER URGENTLY (HIGH) For:
- Any fever in child < 3 months
- Fever with any red flag symptoms
- Fever > 7 days without clear cause
- Fever with meningeal signs

Management:
- Temperature control
- Hydration
- Identify cause
- Treat underlying condition
- Monitor for complications

SECTION 5: SEIZURES IN CHILDREN
===============================

Types:
- Febrile seizures (with fever)
- Epilepsy (recurrent)
- Hypoglycemic seizures
- Infections (meningitis, encephalitis)
- Trauma
- Toxins

During Seizure:
1. Keep calm and stay with child
2. Prevent injury:
   - Remove hard objects
   - Cushion head
   - Loosen tight clothing
   - Do NOT restrain
3. Position:
   - Turn to side if possible
   - Keep airway open
   - Prevent aspiration
4. Note:
   - Time of onset
   - Duration
   - Body parts affected
   - Incontinence
   - Injury

After Seizure:
1. Check breathing and consciousness
2. Monitor vital signs
3. Place in recovery position
4. Monitor for second seizure
5. Do NOT give anything orally until fully conscious

REFER URGENTLY (HIGH) For:
- First-time seizure
- Seizure lasting > 5 minutes
- Multiple seizures without full recovery
- Injury from seizure
- Seizure with fever and stiff neck
- Status epilepticus (continuous seizures)

Management:
- Airway management if needed
- Medication: diazepam (if available) for active seizure
- Treat underlying cause
- Prevent injury
- Monitor recovery

SECTION 6: ACUTE MALARIA IN CHILDREN
====================================

Symptoms:
- High fever (often sudden)
- Chills and sweating
- Headache
- Body aches
- Weakness
- Vomiting
- Diarrhea
- Cough (sometimes)

Severe Malaria (REFER URGENTLY - HIGH):
- Altered consciousness/coma
- Severe anemia (pallor)
- Respiratory distress
- Severe malnutrition
- Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar)
- Acidosis (deep, rapid breathing)
- Acute kidney injury
- Cerebral malaria (seizures, coma)
- Severe bleeding

Assessment:
- Where is child from (endemic area?)
- Any travel to malaria areas?
- Fever pattern?
- Associated symptoms?

Management:
- Immediate anti-malarial treatment
- Treat complications
- Hydration
- Temperature control
- Monitor for severe features

Antimalarial Treatment (depends on region):
- Artemether (injectable)
- Artemisinin-based combinations (oral)
- Quinine (if artemisinin unavailable)

SECTION 7: ACUTE FLACCID PARALYSIS
==================================

Causes:
- Polio
- Guillain-Barré syndrome
- Myasthenia gravis
- Botulism
- Spinal cord injury
- Tick paralysis
- Porphyria
- Hyperkalemia

Assessment:
- Onset (sudden vs gradual)
- Progression (ascending vs descending)
- Symmetry (bilateral vs unilateral)
- Sensory changes
- Associated symptoms

Danger Signs:
- Respiratory muscle involvement (breathing difficulty)
- Bulbar involvement (difficulty swallowing)
- Autonomic involvement (blood pressure, heart rate changes)

REFER URGENTLY (HIGH) For:
- Any acute paralysis
- Respiratory involvement
- Polio suspected (unvaccinated child with flaccid paralysis)
- Associated with fever and neck stiffness

Management:
- Maintain airway
- Respiratory support if needed
- Nutrition support
- Prevent complications
- Identify cause

SECTION 8: SEVERE ACUTE MALNUTRITION (SAM)
===========================================

Signs:
- Weight < 70% of expected for age
- Mid-upper arm circumference < 11.5 cm
- Bilateral pitting edema (kwashiorkor)
- Visible ribs, hip bones, spine

Complications (REFER URGENTLY - HIGH):
- Hypoglycemia
- Severe infections
- Heart failure
- Breathing difficulty
- Severe anemia
- Metabolic derangements

Assessment:
- Nutritional status
- Vaccination status
- Developmental milestone
- Infectious disease status
- Micronutrient deficiencies

Management:
- Nutritional rehabilitation
- Treat infections
- Correct electrolyte abnormalities
- Careful feeding (refeeding syndrome risk)
- Micronutrient supplementation
- Psychosocial support
- Follow-up care

SECTION 9: NEONATAL COMPLICATIONS (First 28 Days)
================================================

Birth Asphyxia:
- Not breathing at birth
- Blue color
- Poor muscle tone
- Poor reflexes

Management:
- Clear airway
- Dry and stimulate
- If no breathing after 10 seconds: start rescue breaths
- Refer URGENTLY - HIGH

Preterm Infant (< 37 weeks):
- Risk of multiple complications
- Respiratory distress
- Difficulty feeding
- Temperature instability
- Infection risk

Management:
- Warmth (skin-to-skin if stable)
- Support feeding
- Monitor closely
- Infection prevention
- Refer if problems

Neonatal Sepsis:
Signs (Can be Subtle):
- Fever or low temperature
- Poor feeding
- Lethargy
- Irritability
- Convulsions
- Bleeding
- Jaundice
- Respiratory distress

REFER URGENTLY (HIGH) For Any Signs of Infection

Prevention:
- Clean delivery
- Cord care
- Clean feeding
- Breastfeeding
- Vaccination

Neonatal Tetanus:
Signs:
- Appears well for first 3-10 days
- Difficulty opening mouth (lockjaw)
- Stiffness
- Convulsions
- Respiratory difficulty

Prevention (Most Important):
- Maternal tetanus vaccination during pregnancy
- Clean delivery
- Clean cord care

SECTION 10: PEDIATRIC EMERGENCY MEDICATIONS
===========================================

Dosing by Weight (common medications):

Paracetamol (for fever/pain):
- 15 mg/kg/dose
- Every 4-6 hours
- Maximum 5 doses/24 hours

Ibuprofen (for fever/pain):
- 10 mg/kg/dose
- Every 6-8 hours
- Maximum 40 mg/kg/day

Amoxicillin (antibiotic):
- 25-45 mg/kg/day divided into 3 doses
- For infections

Metronidazole (anti-parasitic/antibiotic):
- 7.5 mg/kg/dose
- 3 times daily
- For certain infections

Diazepam (for seizures):
- 0.1-0.3 mg/kg/dose IV/IM
- Or 0.5 mg/kg rectally
- Maximum single dose 10 mg

Dextrose 10% (for hypoglycemia):
- 2-5 mL/kg IV if possible
- Or 15-20 grams orally if conscious

Important:
- Calculate dosages carefully
- Use age-appropriate routes
- Monitor for side effects
- Document administration
