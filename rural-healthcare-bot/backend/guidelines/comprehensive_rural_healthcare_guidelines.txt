RURAL HEALTHCARE CLINICAL GUIDELINES
=====================================

SECTION 1: ACUTE FEVER AND COMMON INFECTIONS
==============================================

1.1 FEVER - GENERAL MANAGEMENT

Definition: Body temperature > 38°C (100.4°F)

Causes in Rural Settings:
- Malaria
- Typhoid fever
- Dengue fever
- Common cold
- Flu (Influenza)
- Pneumonia
- Urinary tract infections
- Other viral infections

Assessment:
- Ask about: onset, duration, pattern, associated symptoms
- Check: temperature, respiratory rate, pulse, blood pressure
- Examine: throat, lymph nodes, lungs, abdomen, skin

Red Flag Symptoms (Refer URGENTLY - HIGH):
- High fever (> 40°C) not responding to paracetamol
- Severe headache with stiff neck (possible meningitis)
- Difficulty breathing or chest pain
- Confusion or altered consciousness
- Severe weakness or inability to drink
- Signs of severe dehydration
- Bleeding from gums, nose, or in stool
- Jaundice (yellow eyes/skin)
- Abdominal pain with rigid abdomen

Home Management for Simple Fever:
1. Rest - ensure adequate sleep
2. Hydration - drink plenty of water, ORS, juice
3. Nutrition - light, easy-to-digest food
4. Temperature control:
   - Wear light clothing
   - Use light bedding
   - Sponging with lukewarm water (NOT cold)
5. Medications:
   - Paracetamol 500mg every 4-6 hours (max 3000mg/day)
   - OR Ibuprofen 200-400mg every 6-8 hours (with food)

Fever in Children:
- Strip off excess clothing
- Use tepid sponging (lukewarm water on forehead, armpits, groin)
- Offer frequent fluids
- Paracetamol: 15mg/kg/dose every 4-6 hours (max 5 doses/24 hours)
- Ibuprofen: 10mg/kg/dose every 6-8 hours

When to Refer (MEDIUM Urgency):
- Fever lasting > 7 days
- Fever with persistent cough
- Fever with rash
- Fever in child < 3 months
- Fever in pregnant woman
- Fever with pain in specific body parts
- Fever not improving with home care

1.2 MALARIA

Symptoms:
- Fever (often cyclical - high fever followed by sweating)
- Chills and rigors
- Headache
- Muscle and joint pain
- Weakness and fatigue
- Nausea and vomiting
- Diarrhea

Risk Factors:
- Living in malaria endemic areas
- Poor sanitation
- Stagnant water nearby
- Recent travel to malaria areas

Prevention:
- Sleep under insecticide-treated mosquito net
- Wear long sleeves and pants in evening
- Avoid standing water
- Use mosquito repellent
- Drainage of stagnant water

Diagnosis Clues:
- Cyclical fever pattern (every 48-72 hours)
- History of recent travel or living in malaria area
- Associated with rainy season

When to Refer (HIGH Urgency):
- Severe malaria with complications
- Malaria with breathing difficulty
- Malaria with severe anemia
- Malaria in pregnancy
- Malaria in child < 5 years
- Persistent fever after treatment
- Confusion or seizures

Home Care While Awaiting Referral:
- Rest
- Drink plenty of fluids
- Paracetamol for fever
- Light meals
- Keep mosquito net in place

1.3 TYPHOID FEVER

Symptoms:
- Gradual onset of fever
- Headache
- Body aches
- Loss of appetite
- Sometimes constipation (unlike typical fever)
- Weakness
- In 3rd week: rose spots on trunk, abdominal distension, delirium

Duration: Typically 3-4 weeks if untreated

Transmission:
- Contaminated water
- Poor food handling
- Poor sanitation

Prevention:
- Drink boiled or treated water
- Proper food hygiene
- Good hand hygiene
- Vaccination (if available)
- Sanitation improvements

When to Refer (HIGH Urgency):
- Suspected typhoid (based on symptoms and epidemiology)
- Fever lasting > 7 days
- Complications: intestinal perforation, shock, encephalopathy
- Any severe illness with gradual fever

Home Support While Awaiting Referral:
- Rest complete bed rest
- Fluids - oral rehydration solution
- Soft, nutritious foods
- Paracetamol for fever
- Watch for complications

1.4 DENGUE FEVER

Symptoms (Classic):
- Sudden onset high fever (39-40°C)
- Severe headache
- Retro-orbital pain (pain behind eyes)
- Muscle and bone pain
- Joint pain
- Rash (typically appears 3-4 days after fever onset)
- Mild bleeding (gum bleed, nosebleed)
- Nausea and vomiting

Duration: Usually 5-7 days

Transmission:
- Aedes mosquito (bites during day)
- Urban and semi-urban areas
- More common in rainy season

Red Flags for Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever (Refer URGENTLY - HIGH):
- Fever subsiding but worsening symptoms
- Severe abdominal pain
- Persistent vomiting
- Bleeding (gums, nose, in stool)
- Black/tarry stools
- Lethargy or restlessness
- Cold, clammy skin
- Severe weakness

Home Care for Uncomplicated Dengue:
1. Rest - complete bed rest
2. Hydration - oral rehydration solution, water, juices
3. Fever management - paracetamol (NOT aspirin or ibuprofen)
4. Nutrition - light, easy-to-digest foods
5. Monitoring - watch for warning signs
6. Mosquito protection - keep mosquito net

When to Refer (MEDIUM to HIGH):
- Any bleeding manifestations
- Severe abdominal pain
- Persistent vomiting
- Lethargy or restlessness
- Fever lasting > 7 days
- Worsening of symptoms after fever subsides


SECTION 2: RESPIRATORY INFECTIONS
==================================

2.1 COMMON COLD

Symptoms:
- Runny or blocked nose
- Sneezing
- Sore throat
- Mild cough
- Low-grade fever (may or may not be present)
- Mild headache
- General malaise

Cause: Viral (usually rhinovirus)

Duration: 3-7 days typically

Home Management:
1. Rest - adequate sleep
2. Fluids - water, warm tea, soup
3. Throat care:
   - Gargle with salt water (1/2 tsp salt in warm water)
   - Throat lozenges
   - Honey (soothing)
4. Nasal care:
   - Saline nasal drops/spray
   - Elevate head while sleeping
5. Fever/pain - paracetamol as needed
6. Avoid irritants - smoke, dust

When to Refer (MEDIUM):
- Worsening after 7 days
- Fever > 39°C
- Severe sore throat with difficulty swallowing
- Cough with colored sputum
- Any breathing difficulty
- Signs of pneumonia

2.2 ACUTE COUGH

Assessment:
Duration:
- Acute cough: < 2 weeks
- Subacute cough: 2-8 weeks
- Chronic cough: > 8 weeks

Associated Symptoms:
- Sputum production (productive vs dry)
- Fever
- Breathing difficulty
- Chest pain
- Hemoptysis (coughing blood)

Causes:
- Common cold
- Bronchitis
- Pneumonia
- Whooping cough
- Asthma
- TB (if prolonged)
- Aspiration

Red Flags (Refer URGENTLY - HIGH):
- Severe breathing difficulty
- Chest pain with breathing
- Coughing blood
- Blue lips or fingernails
- Altered mental status
- Very high fever
- Severe weakness

Home Care for Simple Cough:
1. Hydration - water, warm fluids
2. Humidity - steam inhalation
3. Elevated position - sleep with head raised
4. Throat soothing:
   - Honey and lemon in warm water
   - Ginger tea
   - Throat lozenges
5. Avoid irritants - smoke, dust
6. Cough suppressants:
   - Dextromethorphan for dry cough
   - Expectorants for productive cough
7. Paracetamol if fever

When to Refer (MEDIUM):
- Persistent cough > 2 weeks
- Productive cough with colored/bloody sputum
- Associated fever > 38.5°C
- Breathing difficulty
- Chest pain
- Cough not improving in 5-7 days
- Patient age < 2 years or > 60 years
- History of TB exposure

2.3 PNEUMONIA

Symptoms:
- Cough (productive, with greenish/yellowish sputum)
- Fever (often high)
- Chest pain (worse with breathing)
- Shortness of breath
- Rapid breathing
- General weakness

Risk Factors:
- Recent upper respiratory infection
- Smoking
- Alcohol abuse
- Bedridden patients
- Elderly
- Children < 5 years
- Chronic diseases (diabetes, heart disease)

Danger Signs (Refer URGENTLY - HIGH):
- Difficulty breathing
- Chest indrawing (visible drawing in of ribs/abdomen)
- Nasal flaring
- Stridor (noisy breathing)
- Very high fever (> 39.5°C)
- Altered consciousness
- Inability to drink or persistent vomiting
- Cyanosis (blue lips/fingernails)

Home Care While Awaiting Referral:
1. Position - semi-upright position to ease breathing
2. Hydration - encourage fluid intake
3. Fever - paracetamol
4. Oxygen if available
5. Monitor breathing rate and signs of deterioration

2.4 WHOOPING COUGH (Pertussis)

Symptoms:
- Starts like common cold (runny nose, sneezing)
- Dry cough develops in 1-2 weeks
- After 2 weeks, characteristic "whoop" sound during inspiration
- Coughing fits, often worse at night
- Vomiting after cough
- Exhaustion
- Fever usually absent

Duration: Can last 6-8 weeks or longer

High Risk Complications:
- Infants < 6 months
- Unvaccinated children

Red Flags (Refer URGENTLY - HIGH):
- Infant with whooping cough
- Severe coughing fits
- Difficulty breathing
- Cyanosis during cough
- Convulsions
- Vomiting and weight loss
- Respiratory distress

Prevention:
- Vaccination (DPT vaccine)
- Avoiding contact with infected persons
- Droplet precautions

Isolation:
- First 5 days of antibiotic treatment
- Keep away from infants

Home Care While Awaiting Treatment:
- Isolation from others
- Monitor breathing
- Ensure nutrition despite cough
- Keep in upright position
- Calm environment


SECTION 3: GASTROINTESTINAL CONDITIONS
=======================================

3.1 ACUTE DIARRHEA

Definition: Passage of 3 or more loose/watery stools in 24 hours

Causes:
- Contaminated food/water
- Poor hygiene
- Viral infection
- Bacterial infection
- Parasitic infection

Assessment:
- Duration and frequency
- Consistency (watery, bloody)
- Associated symptoms (fever, vomiting, abdominal pain)
- Dehydration status
- Recent travel/food

Dehydration Signs:
Mild-Moderate:
- Increased thirst
- Dry mouth
- Decreased urine
- Mild lethargy

Severe (Refer URGENTLY - HIGH):
- Extreme thirst
- Very dry mouth and tongue
- Little or no urine
- Severe lethargy or confusion
- Sunken eyes
- Weak pulse
- Low blood pressure
- Skin tenting (pinched skin returns slowly)

Red Flag Symptoms (Refer URGENTLY - HIGH):
- Severe dehydration signs
- Blood in stool
- Severe abdominal pain
- High fever (> 39°C)
- Vomiting everything, cannot keep fluids down
- Signs of septic shock
- Age < 6 months
- Elderly or chronic illness

Home Management - ORAL REHYDRATION (Most Important):

ORS Recipe (if not available):
- 1 liter clean water
- 6 teaspoons sugar
- 1/2 teaspoon salt
- Juice or flavoring (optional)

ORS Administration:
- Frequent small amounts (5-10 mL every few minutes)
- After each stool: 10 mL/kg ORS
- Continue until urine volume normal

Foods to Give (Bland Diet):
- Rice
- Bread
- Bananas
- Potatoes
- Boiled vegetables
- Chicken or fish (well-cooked)

AVOID:
- Dairy products
- High fat foods
- High fiber foods
- Sugary drinks
- Spicy foods

Medications:
- Antidiarrheals (loperamide) - AVOID in bloody diarrhea or high fever
- Antibiotics - only if bloody diarrhea or specific bacterial cause

When to Refer (MEDIUM):
- Diarrhea lasting > 14 days
- Blood in stool
- Severe abdominal pain
- Signs of dehydration despite ORS
- Unable to drink or frequent vomiting
- Very high fever
- Age < 1 year

3.2 CHOLERA

Symptoms:
- Sudden onset watery diarrhea ("rice-water" stools)
- Vomiting
- Rapid dehydration
- Weakness
- Muscle cramps
- Low blood pressure
- Shock if untreated

Transmission:
- Contaminated water
- Poor sanitation

Prevention:
- Safe drinking water
- Food hygiene
- Hand hygiene
- Sanitation
- Vaccination (if available)

Urgent Referral (HIGH):
- Suspected cholera
- Signs of severe dehydration
- Signs of shock

Home Care While Awaiting Referral:
- Immediate aggressive rehydration with ORS
- IV fluids needed at referral center
- Continue feeding if tolerated
- Isolation from others

3.3 ABDOMINAL PAIN

Assessment:
- Location of pain
- Character (sharp, dull, cramping)
- Onset and duration
- Associated symptoms
- Recent meals, injuries, bowel habits

Causes (Common):
- Gastritis/indigestion
- Constipation
- Diarrhea
- Food poisoning
- Worms
- Urinary/kidney issues
- Appendicitis
- Intestinal obstruction

Red Flags Requiring Urgent Referral (HIGH):
- Severe pain (patient in distress)
- Rigid abdomen
- Abdominal distension with pain
- Persistent vomiting
- Blood in vomit or stool
- Pain after trauma
- Signs of shock
- Fever with severe pain
- Pain in pregnant woman

Home Care for Mild Abdominal Pain:
1. Rest lying down
2. Warm compress on abdomen
3. Light diet
4. Avoid dairy, fatty foods
5. Hydration with water, ORS
6. Antacids if indigestion suspected
7. Observe for worsening

When to Refer (MEDIUM):
- Pain lasting > 24 hours
- Increasing pain
- Associated with fever
- Vomiting persisting > 4 hours
- Diarrhea with severe pain
- Unresponsive to home care


SECTION 4: MATERNAL AND CHILD HEALTH
====================================

4.1 PREGNANCY-RELATED COMPLICATIONS

Danger Signs in Pregnancy (Refer URGENTLY - HIGH):
- Vaginal bleeding
- Severe abdominal pain
- Severe headache with vision changes
- Swelling of face and hands
- Convulsions/seizures
- Unconsciousness
- Difficulty breathing
- Chest pain
- Fever with chills
- Foul-smelling vaginal discharge
- Severe weakness/dizziness
- Loss of baby movement (after 5 months)

First Trimester (0-13 weeks):
Miscarriage signs:
- Vaginal bleeding
- Abdominal cramps
- Passage of tissue

Ectopic pregnancy (implantation outside uterus):
- Severe abdominal pain (often one-sided)
- Vaginal bleeding
- Shoulder pain
- Dizziness/fainting

REFER URGENTLY for any bleeding or severe pain

Second Trimester (14-26 weeks):
- Preterm labor signs: vaginal bleeding, fluid leakage, contractions
- Infections: fever, discharge, pain
REFER URGENTLY

Third Trimester (27 weeks-delivery):
Pre-eclampsia/Eclampsia Signs:
- High blood pressure
- Swelling of face, hands, feet
- Severe headache
- Vision changes
- Epigastric pain (upper abdomen)
- Seizures (eclampsia)
REFER URGENTLY - HIGH

Preterm Labor:
- Regular contractions < 37 weeks
- Vaginal bleeding
- Fluid leakage
- Pelvic pressure
REFER URGENTLY - HIGH

Labor Complications:
- Prolonged labor (> 12 hours, no progress)
- Excessive bleeding
- Signs of fetal distress
- Cord prolapse (cord coming out first)
REFER URGENTLY - HIGH

4.2 POSTPARTUM COMPLICATIONS (After Delivery)

Danger Signs (Refer URGENTLY - HIGH):
- Excessive bleeding (> 500 mL or soaking pads)
- Fever > 38°C
- Severe abdominal pain
- Foul-smelling discharge
- Severe headache or vision changes
- Breathing difficulty
- Chest pain
- Seizures
- Confusion
- Inability to urinate
- Signs of blood clot (leg swelling, pain)

Postpartum Hemorrhage:
- Most common cause of maternal death
- Can occur within hours of delivery
- Requires urgent blood transfusion and treatment
REFER IMMEDIATELY - HIGH

Postpartum Infection (Sepsis):
- Fever
- Foul-smelling discharge
- Abdominal tenderness
- Chills
- Rapid pulse
REFER URGENTLY - HIGH

4.3 NEWBORN CARE (First 28 Days)

Danger Signs (Refer URGENTLY - HIGH):
- Not eating or difficulty feeding
- Jaundice (yellow color) on face/body in first 24 hours
- Persistent jaundice beyond 2 weeks
- Fever > 38°C
- Difficulty breathing (fast breathing, indrawing)
- Chest indrawing
- Stridor
- Convulsions
- Severe lethargy/unresponsiveness
- Severe diarrhea or vomiting
- Umbilical cord redness, swelling, discharge
- Bleeding from umbilical cord
- Swelling of fontanelle (soft spot on head)
- Skin boils or pustules spreading
- Inability to pass urine or stool

Cord Care:
- Keep dry and clean
- Do NOT apply harmful substances (ash, oil, etc.)
- Observe for infection signs
- Umbilicus should separate by day 7-14

Jaundice in Newborns:
- Common in first 3-5 days (physiological)
- Watch for excessive jaundice (dark urine, pale stools)
- Excessive jaundice can cause brain damage
- Refer if severe or persistent

Feeding:
- Exclusive breastfeeding for first 6 months if possible
- Proper latch is important
- Newborn should feed 8-12 times per 24 hours
- Watch for adequate milk transfer

Home Care:
- Keep warm (skin-to-skin contact)
- Hygiene for umbilical cord
- Daily gentle bathing
- Regular feeding
- Sleep in safe place (not on soft surfaces)
- Immediate referral for danger signs

4.4 CHILD ILLNESS (1 Month - 5 Years)

General Danger Signs in Children (Refer URGENTLY - HIGH):
- Difficulty breathing or fast breathing
- Chest indrawing
- Stridor
- Severe malnutrition or wasting
- Severe dehydration
- Lethargy or unresponsiveness
- Unconsciousness
- Convulsions/seizures
- Neck stiffness
- Rash (petechial or purpuric)
- Severe abdominal distension or pain
- Persistent vomiting
- Bloody diarrhea
- Blood in urine or stool
- Paleness or cyanosis (blue color)
- Severe anemia
- Hypothermia (low body temperature)
- Severe malaria
- Severe pneumonia
- Very high fever (> 40°C) not responding to treatment

Dehydration in Children (Refer URGENTLY if SEVERE - HIGH):
Mild-Moderate:
- Increased thirst
- Dry mucous membranes
- Decreased tears
- Decreased urine output
- Skin turgor slightly reduced

Severe:
- Extreme thirst or lethargy
- Very dry mouth
- No tears
- Sunken eyes
- Skin tenting
- Weak pulse
- Low blood pressure
- Altered consciousness

Management:
- ORS: 50-100 mL/kg over 4 hours
- Reassess and continue as needed

Malnutrition:
- Height/weight below normal for age
- Visible ribs, hip bones, spine
- Edema (swelling) in feet
- Loose, dry skin
- Behavioral changes

Refer for nutritional rehabilitation

Common Infections:
- Respiratory tract infections
- Diarrhea/gastroenteritis
- Malaria
- Measles
- Whooping cough
- Meningitis

Vaccination:
- Follow national immunization schedule
- Keep vaccination records
- Complete all vaccines for optimal protection


SECTION 5: COMMON PARASITIC INFECTIONS
======================================

5.1 HELMINTH (WORM) INFECTIONS

Types:
- Roundworms (Ascaris)
- Hookworms
- Tapeworms
- Whipworms

Symptoms:
- Abdominal pain
- Diarrhea or constipation
- Anemia
- Malnutrition
- Itching around anus
- Visible worms in stool
- General weakness

Risk Factors:
- Poor sanitation
- Bare foot walking (hookworms)
- Poor food hygiene
- Contaminated water

Prevention:
- Proper sanitation (latrine use)
- Wearing shoes
- Hand hygiene before eating
- Food hygiene
- Water treatment

Treatment:
- Deworming medications (mebendazole, albendazole)
- Often available at community health centers
- May be given periodically to all children

5.2 GIARDIASIS AND AMEBIASIS

Symptoms:
- Diarrhea (sometimes greasy/fatty)
- Abdominal cramps
- Flatulence and bloating
- Weight loss
- General weakness

Transmission:
- Contaminated water
- Poor hygiene

Prevention:
- Clean drinking water
- Hand hygiene
- Food hygiene
- Sanitation

Treatment:
- Specific medications needed
- Refer if symptoms persist

5.3 SCHISTOSOMIASIS

Risk:
- Contact with contaminated freshwater
- Bathing/swimming in affected areas

Symptoms:
- Acute: fever, rash, itching
- Chronic: anemia, malnutrition, weakness

Prevention:
- Avoid contaminated water
- Proper sanitation


SECTION 6: CHRONIC CONDITIONS MANAGEMENT
==========================================

6.1 HYPERTENSION (High Blood Pressure)

Normal: < 120/80 mmHg
Elevated: 120-129/<80
Stage 1: 130-139/80-89
Stage 2: ≥ 140/90

Symptoms (Often None):
- Sometimes: headache, dizziness, chest discomfort
- Often called "silent killer"

Risk Factors:
- Age > 45 years (men), > 55 (women)
- Family history
- Obesity
- High salt intake
- Sedentary lifestyle
- Smoking
- Excessive alcohol
- Stress
- Diabetes
- Chronic kidney disease

Danger Signs (Refer URGENTLY - HIGH):
- Severe headache with fever
- Chest pain or pressure
- Difficulty breathing
- Severe vision changes
- Severe dizziness
- Confused or altered consciousness
- Blood pressure > 180/120 with symptoms
- Weakness/numbness of face, arm, leg (stroke signs)

Home Management:
1. Regular blood pressure monitoring
2. Lifestyle changes:
   - Reduce salt intake
   - Healthy diet (fruits, vegetables)
   - Regular exercise (150 min/week)
   - Weight loss if overweight
   - Limit alcohol
   - Stress management
   - Sleep well
   - Avoid tobacco
3. Medications:
   - Take as prescribed
   - Don't stop suddenly
   - Keep records of BP readings

When to Refer:
- BP consistently > 160/100 despite lifestyle changes
- BP readings > 180/120
- Any symptoms of hypertension complications
- Difficult to control BP
- Side effects from medications

6.2 DIABETES MELLITUS

Type 2 (Common in Rural Areas):
Symptoms:
- Increased thirst
- Frequent urination
- Increased hunger
- Unexplained weight loss
- Fatigue
- Blurred vision
- Slow healing wounds

Risk Factors:
- Family history
- Obesity
- Sedentary lifestyle
- Age > 45 years
- Gestational diabetes history

Danger Signs (Refer URGENTLY - HIGH):
- Diabetic ketoacidosis: fruity breath, rapid breathing, confusion
- Hypoglycemia: confusion, sweating, rapid heartbeat, anxiety
- Severe infections/wounds
- Vision loss
- Severe abdominal pain
- Chest pain
- Difficulty breathing

Home Management:
1. Blood sugar monitoring (if possible)
2. Diet:
   - Regular meals
   - Whole grains
   - Vegetables
   - Limit sugar/sweets
   - Portion control
3. Exercise: 150 minutes moderate activity/week
4. Weight management
5. Medications: as prescribed
6. Foot care:
   - Daily inspection for wounds
   - Keep clean and dry
   - Wear proper shoes
   - See healthcare worker for corns/calluses
7. Oral health: regular brushing, dental care

When to Refer (MEDIUM):
- Blood sugar consistently high (> 250 mg/dL)
- Any signs of complications
- Suspected hypoglycemia
- Infections not healing
- New vision problems
- Symptoms of kidney disease
- Chest pain
- Severe headache

6.3 ASTHMA

Symptoms:
- Recurrent coughing (especially at night, during play)
- Wheezing (whistling sound while breathing)
- Shortness of breath
- Chest tightness
- Fatigue

Triggers:
- Dust, smoke
- Cold air
- Exercise
- Infections
- Allergens
- Strong emotions
- Air pollution

Asthma Attack Signs (Refer URGENTLY - HIGH):
- Severe difficulty breathing
- Inability to speak full sentences
- Severe wheezing or no sound at all
- Pale or blue lips
- Panic or confusion
- Rapid, weak pulse
- Unconsciousness

Home Management:
1. Identify and avoid triggers
2. Keep rescue inhaler available
3. Use medications as prescribed:
   - Quick-relief inhalers (blue) for acute symptoms
   - Maintenance inhalers (usually brown/orange) daily
4. Peak flow monitoring (if available)
5. Avoid smoke, dust, pollution
6. Regular exercise appropriate to condition
7. Keep home clean and dry

When to Refer (MEDIUM to HIGH):
- Frequent asthma attacks
- Symptoms not controlled with medications
- Difficulty using inhaler
- Suspected asthma diagnosis
- Any acute severe attack

6.4 CHRONIC OBSTRUCTIVE PULMONARY DISEASE (COPD)

Risk Factors:
- Smoking
- Indoor air pollution (cooking fires, biomass)
- Occupational exposures
- Chronic respiratory infections

Symptoms:
- Persistent cough
- Sputum production
- Shortness of breath
- Wheezing
- Chest tightness
- Fatigue

Red Flags (Refer URGENTLY - HIGH):
- Severe breathing difficulty
- Severe cough with blood
- Altered consciousness
- Severe weakness
- Blue lips or fingernails
- Edema of legs

Home Management:
1. Smoking cessation (if applicable)
2. Reduce air pollution exposure
3. Breathing exercises
4. Medications: as prescribed
5. Vaccination: influenza, pneumococcal
6. Activity as tolerated
7. Nutrition support

When to Refer (MEDIUM):
- Worsening symptoms
- Persistent cough
- Exacerbations
- Suspected COPD diagnosis


SECTION 7: INJURIES AND ACCIDENTS
=================================

7.1 MINOR WOUNDS AND CUTS

Management:
1. Clean hands first
2. Wash wound with clean water
3. Remove debris if necessary
4. Control bleeding:
   - Apply direct pressure with clean cloth
   - Elevate if possible
   - Continue pressure for 5-10 minutes
5. Wash with soap and clean water
6. Dry with clean cloth
7. Apply antibiotic ointment
8. Cover with sterile dressing if needed
9. Keep clean and dry
10. Remove dressing daily, inspect, reapply

When to Refer (MEDIUM):
- Deep cuts (gaping edges)
- Cuts on face or joints
- Cuts from dirty objects
- Animal bites
- Cut with bleeding that won't stop after 15 minutes
- Signs of infection (redness, warmth, pus, swelling)

7.2 BURNS

Classification:
Superficial (1st degree):
- Red skin, no blistering
- Pain

Partial thickness (2nd degree):
- Blistering
- Erythema (redness)
- Severe pain

Full thickness (3rd degree):
- Charred or white appearance
- No pain sensation
- Loss of function

Management of Minor Burns (< 5% body surface):
1. Cool immediately:
   - Cool running water for 5-20 minutes
   - Do NOT use ice
2. Remove restrictive jewelry/clothing
3. Dry with clean cloth
4. Do NOT apply toothpaste, oil, butter
5. Apply antibiotic ointment
6. Cover with sterile non-adherent dressing
7. Pain relief: paracetamol, ibuprofen
8. Tetanus prophylaxis if needed
9. Monitor for infection

When to Refer URGENTLY (HIGH):
- Burns > 10% body surface (any depth)
- Any full-thickness burn
- Burns of face, hands, genitals, joints
- Circumferential burns (around limb)
- Inhalation injury (stridor, singed nasal hair, carbonaceous sputum)
- Chemical burns
- Electrical burns
- Burns in children < 5 years or elderly

7.3 FRACTURES AND SPRAINS

Fracture Signs:
- Severe pain
- Swelling
- Deformity
- Inability to use limb
- Crepitus (crackling sensation)

Sprain Signs:
- Pain
- Swelling
- Bruising
- Limited movement
- Usually less severe than fracture

First Aid for Fractures (Refer URGENTLY - HIGH):
1. Immobilize:
   - Make sling for arm
   - Splint leg (using cardboard, padding)
   - Keep still
2. Ice/cold: first 24 hours
3. Elevation: reduce swelling
4. Analgesia: paracetamol, ibuprofen
5. Tetanus: if skin broken

Home Care for Sprains:
- RICE protocol:
  - Rest: immobilize for first 24-48 hours
  - Ice: 15-20 minutes every 3 hours for first 48 hours
  - Compression: elastic bandage
  - Elevation: above heart level
- Analgesia: paracetamol, ibuprofen
- Gradual mobilization after acute phase

When to Refer (MEDIUM to HIGH):
- Suspected fractures
- Severe sprains
- Unable to bear weight or use limb
- Severe swelling
- Deformity
- Fractures in children
- Fractures near joints

7.4 HEAD INJURIES

Mild (No Loss of Consciousness):
- Headache
- Mild dizziness
- Slight confusion

Severe Signs (Refer URGENTLY - HIGH):
- Loss of consciousness
- Altered consciousness
- Vomiting (multiple times)
- Severe headache
- Seizures
- Bleeding from ears, nose, mouth
- Clear fluid from nose/ears
- Severe dizziness
- Inability to remember events

Home Care for Mild Head Injury:
1. Rest lying down
2. Ice pack to head (20 minutes at a time)
3. Observation every hour for 24 hours
4. Pain relief: paracetamol
5. Watch for danger signs

Observation Points:
- Consciousness level
- Breathing pattern
- Pupil size and reaction
- Vomiting
- Seizures
- Confusion or behavior changes

When to Refer (MEDIUM to HIGH):
- Any loss of consciousness
- Vomiting after injury
- Seizures
- Severe headache
- Any danger signs
- Multiple head injuries
- Elderly or on blood thinners

7.5 ANIMAL BITES

Management:
1. Wash wound:
   - Copious amounts of water
   - With soap
   - For at least 5 minutes
2. Control bleeding
3. Dry with clean cloth
4. Antibiotic ointment
5. Dressing if needed
6. Assess rabies risk:
   - Type of animal (dog, cat, bat, etc.)
   - Was it provoked?
   - Was it vaccinated (if known)?
7. Tetanus prophylaxis
8. Rabies post-exposure prophylaxis:
   - Rabies vaccination series (if high risk)
   - Rabies immunoglobulin (if high risk)

Refer URGENTLY (HIGH) for:
- Animal bites
- Facial bites
- Bites over joints
- Deep wounds
- Possible rabies exposure
- Any bite that looks infected


SECTION 8: SKIN CONDITIONS
==========================

8.1 COMMON SKIN INFECTIONS

Bacterial Infections:
- Impetigo:
  - Honey-crusted sores
  - Contagious
  - Often in children
  - Treatment: antibacterial cream/ointment
  - Oral antibiotics if widespread
  
- Cellulitis:
  - Spreading redness, warmth
  - Swelling, pain
  - Sometimes fever
  - Refer URGENTLY - requires IV antibiotics

Fungal Infections:
- Ringworm (Tinea):
  - Red circular patches
  - Itching
  - Common in children
  - Spread by contact
  - Treatment: antifungal cream
  - Oral medication if severe or resistant

- Candidiasis:
  - White patches/plaques
  - May have red, irritated base
  - Itching/burning
  - Treatment: antifungal cream or tablets

Parasitic:
- Scabies:
  - Itching (worse at night)
  - Burrows (fine lines on skin)
  - Common in children
  - Spread by contact
  - Treatment: scabicide lotion
  - Treat household members

- Lice:
  - Itching
  - Visible lice or nits
  - Treatment: insecticidal shampoo
  - Comb out nits

8.2 ECZEMA (Dermatitis)

Symptoms:
- Itching (intense, often worse at night)
- Dry, sensitive skin
- Red, inflamed skin
- Small raised bumps
- Blisters and oozing if severe
- Cracked, swollen, darkened skin

Triggers:
- Soap, detergents
- Water exposure
- Heat, sweating
- Stress
- Dust, pollen
- Certain fabrics

Home Care:
1. Moisturize:
   - Apply lotion/cream frequently
   - Use immediately after bathing
2. Bathing:
   - Use lukewarm water
   - Short baths/showers
   - Gentle cleanser
   - Pat dry
3. Avoid triggers
4. Use soft clothing
5. Manage stress
6. Antihistamines for itching
7. Topical corticosteroid cream if available

When to Refer (MEDIUM):
- Severe symptoms
- Signs of infection
- Not responding to home care
- Affecting daily activities
- Widespread involvement

8.3 ALLERGIC REACTIONS AND HIVES

Mild Reaction:
- Itching
- Rash (hives)
- Mild swelling

Treatment:
- Remove allergen if possible
- Cool compresses
- Antihistamine (cetirizine, chlorpheniramine)
- Hydrocortisone cream for itching

Severe Reaction (Anaphylaxis - Refer URGENTLY - HIGH):
- Difficulty breathing
- Throat swelling
- Lip/tongue swelling
- Severe itching/hives
- Dizziness
- Weakness
- Shock
- Loss of consciousness

Common Allergens:
- Foods: peanuts, seafood, milk, eggs
- Medications: antibiotics
- Insect stings/bites
- Latex
- Chemicals


SECTION 9: PREVENTION AND HEALTH PROMOTION
===========================================

9.1 VACCINATION

Routine Immunizations (Follow National Schedule):
- BCG (Tuberculosis): Birth
- Polio: 6, 10, 14 weeks + boosters
- DPT (Diphtheria, Pertussis, Tetanus): 6, 10, 14 weeks + boosters
- Hepatitis B: Birth + schedule
- Hib (Haemophilus influenzae): 6, 10, 14 weeks
- PCV (Pneumococcal): Schedule varies
- MMR (Measles, Mumps, Rubella): 9-12 months
- Varicella (Chickenpox): varies
- Typhoid: varies
- Japanese Encephalitis: varies (endemic areas)

Adults:
- Tetanus booster every 10 years
- Influenza annually
- Pneumococcal for elderly/chronic conditions
- HPV vaccine for adolescents

Special Populations:
- Pregnant women: Tetanus during pregnancy
- Healthcare workers: Hepatitis B, influenza
- Travelers: Depends on destination

Importance:
- Prevents serious diseases
- Reduces complications
- Protects community
- Cost-effective

9.2 HYGIENE AND SANITATION

Hand Hygiene:
- Wash before eating
- After toilet
- After handling dirt/garbage
- Before preparing food
- After caring for sick person

Technique:
1. Wet hands with clean water
2. Apply soap
3. Rub hands for 20 seconds:
   - Palms
   - Between fingers
   - Back of hands
   - Thumbs
   - Under nails
4. Rinse well
5. Dry with clean cloth

Sanitation:
- Use proper latrine/toilet
- Cover human waste
- Proper disposal of garbage
- Keep water clean
- Separate water for drinking/cooking/washing
- Clean kitchen and food storage
- Animal waste management

Water Safety:
- Use treated/boiled water for drinking
- Boiling: 1 minute at rolling boil
- Chemical treatment: chlorination
- Filtration: sand filters
- Storage: covered containers

Food Hygiene:
- Keep raw and cooked foods separate
- Cook food thoroughly
- Avoid raw animal products unless safe
- Keep food covered
- Clean utensils
- Don't leave food at room temperature > 2 hours

9.3 NUTRITION

Balanced Diet Components:
1. Carbohydrates (45-65% calories):
   - Rice, wheat, millet, corn
   - Vegetables
   - Legumes
   - Avoid refined sugars

2. Protein (10-35% calories):
   - Beans, lentils
   - Nuts, seeds
   - Eggs
   - Poultry, fish
   - Milk products

3. Fat (20-35% calories):
   - Use healthy oils
   - Limit saturated fat
   - Avoid trans fats

4. Vitamins and Minerals:
   - Leafy vegetables (Iron, Calcium, A, C, K)
   - Orange/red vegetables (Vitamin A)
   - Fruits (Vitamin C, fiber)
   - Dairy (Calcium, Protein)
   - Nuts/seeds (Minerals)

Special Needs:
- Pregnant women: Extra calories, iron, calcium, folic acid
- Breastfeeding: Extra calories, nutrients
- Children: Adequate growth nutrition
- Elderly: Adequate nutrition, easy to digest

Undernutrition Signs:
- Low weight for height/age
- Muscle wasting
- Delayed growth/development
- Frequent infections
- Pale appearance
- Edema
- Behavior changes

9.4 PHYSICAL ACTIVITY

Benefits:
- Strengthens heart, muscles, bones
- Improves mental health
- Reduces chronic disease risk
- Helps maintain healthy weight
- Improves sleep

Recommendations:
- Adults: 150 minutes moderate activity/week
- Children: 60 minutes daily
- Include strength training 2x/week

Activities:
- Brisk walking
- Running
- Cycling
- Swimming
- Gardening
- Sports
- Farming/manual work

9.5 INFECTIOUS DISEASE PREVENTION

Respiratory:
- Cover cough/sneeze with elbow
- Keep distance from sick people
- Ventilate rooms
- Avoid smoke

Waterborne:
- Safe water for drinking
- Proper sanitation
- Food hygiene

Bloodborne:
- Safe disposal of needles
- Avoid sharing sharp objects
- Practice safe injection procedures
- Healthcare worker precautions

Vector-borne (Malaria, Dengue):
- Mosquito nets
- Avoid mosquito breeding sites
- Insect repellent
- Long sleeves in evening
- Vaccination if available

Sexual Health:
- Barrier methods
- Regular STI testing
- Communication with partners
- Vaccination (HPV)


SECTION 10: FIRST AID AND EMERGENCY PROCEDURES
===============================================

10.1 BASIC LIFE SUPPORT

When to Start CPR:
- Unconscious
- Not breathing
- No pulse

CPR Procedure:
1. Check responsiveness and breathing
2. Call for help
3. Position person on firm surface
4. Head tilt, chin lift to open airway
5. Start chest compressions:
   - Place heel of one hand on center of chest
   - Place other hand on top
   - Push hard and fast (100-120 compressions per minute)
   - Compress at least 2 inches (5 cm) depth
6. After 30 compressions, give 2 rescue breaths:
   - Open airway
   - Pinch nose closed
   - Seal lips around mouth
   - Give 2 full breaths
7. Continue CPR until:
   - Person wakes up
   - Pulse returns
   - Emergency help arrives
   - You become exhausted

10.2 CHOKING

Signs:
- Inability to speak or cry
- Weak or no cough
- Inability to breathe
- Skin turning blue

For Conscious Adult/Older Child:
1. Ask "Are you choking?" (If nods, proceed)
2. Stand behind person
3. Make fist above navel, below ribcage
4. Grasp fist with other hand
5. Perform quick upward thrusts
6. Repeat until object comes out or person becomes unconscious

For Conscious Infant (< 1 year):
- Alternate back blows and chest thrusts

For Unconscious Person:
- Begin CPR
- Check mouth for object after 30 compressions
- Remove if visible
- Continue CPR

10.3 SHOCK

Signs:
- Cold, clammy skin
- Pale appearance
- Rapid, weak pulse
- Rapid breathing
- Weak or confused
- Nausea
- Thirst

Causes:
- Severe bleeding
- Heart problems
- Severe infections
- Severe allergic reaction
- Dehydration
- Trauma

Management:
1. Call for emergency help
2. Lay person down
3. Elevate legs (unless head/chest injury)
4. Keep warm with blanket
5. Do not give food/drink
6. Monitor breathing and pulse
7. Treat underlying cause if possible
8. Provide reassurance

10.4 SEVERE BLEEDING

Management:
1. Safety first: wear gloves if available
2. Apply direct pressure:
   - Use clean cloth
   - Press firmly on wound
   - Do NOT remove first cloth (add more if needed)
   - Continue for 5-10 minutes
3. Elevate if possible
4. Apply pressure points if limb bleeding:
   - Inner arm for arm bleeding
   - Groin for leg bleeding
5. Apply tourniquet if life-threatening limb bleeding:
   - 2-3 inches above wound
   - Mark time applied
   - Remove only in hospital
6. Treat for shock
7. Seek emergency care

10.5 POISONING

Common Toxins:
- Medications (overdose)
- Pesticides
- Chemicals
- Heavy metals
- Corrosives
- Plants/mushrooms
- Alcohol

Management:
1. Call poison control/emergency
2. Remove from source if safe
3. Do NOT induce vomiting (may worsen)
4. Try to identify poison
5. Give activated charcoal if available (some poisons only)
6. Monitor breathing and consciousness
7. Treat symptoms as they develop
8. Keep sample/container for identification

10.6 ENVIRONMENTAL EMERGENCIES

Heat Stroke:
- High body temperature
- No sweating
- Confusion, altered consciousness
- Hot, dry skin
- Rapid pulse
Management:
- Move to cool place
- Cool body (cool water, ice packs to armpits/groin)
- Fluids if conscious
- Refer URGENTLY

Heat Exhaustion:
- Heavy sweating
- Weakness, dizziness
- Nausea
- Normal or slightly high temperature
Management:
- Move to cool place
- Remove excess clothing
- Cool body
- Sip water

Hypothermia (Low Body Temperature):
- Shivering
- Confusion
- Slurred speech
- Slow pulse and breathing
Management:
- Move to warm place
- Remove wet clothing
- Gradual warming
- Warm fluids if conscious
- Refer URGENTLY

Dehydration:
Signs:
- Excessive thirst
- Dry mouth
- Weakness
- Headache
- Dark urine
- Rapid pulse
Management:
- Oral fluids: water, ORS
- Monitor urine output
- Rest
- Refer if severe

Hypothermia:
Prevention:
- Appropriate clothing for weather
- Shelter from elements
- Regular meals and fluids
