MATERNAL AND WOMEN'S HEALTH GUIDELINES
=====================================

SECTION 1: PREGNANCY COMPLICATIONS
=================================

1.1 VAGINAL BLEEDING IN PREGNANCY

First Trimester (< 13 weeks):

Threatened Miscarriage:
- Vaginal bleeding with closed cervix
- Cramping may be present
- Fetal heart may or may not be present

Incomplete Miscarriage:
- Bleeding with some tissue expelled
- Open cervix
- Cramping may be severe

Complete Miscarriage:
- All products expelled
- Bleeding lessens
- Cramping resolves

Ectopic Pregnancy (Tubal):
- Vaginal bleeding
- Unilateral abdominal pain (often severe)
- Shoulder pain
- Signs of shock if rupture
REFER URGENTLY (HIGH) - Life threatening

Management:
- Assess amount of bleeding
- Check vital signs
- Check for shock signs
- Keep nothing by mouth
- IV access if possible
- REFER URGENTLY (HIGH)

Second Trimester Bleeding:
- May indicate miscarriage
- Preterm labor
- Placental issues
- Infection

REFER URGENTLY (MEDIUM to HIGH)

Third Trimester Bleeding:
Placenta Previa:
- Painless vaginal bleeding
- Bleeding increases with activity
- May be life-threatening
REFER URGENTLY (HIGH)

Placental Abruption:
- Vaginal bleeding or concealed bleeding
- Severe abdominal pain
- Tender uterus
- Signs of shock
- Signs of fetal distress
REFER URGENTLY (HIGH) - Emergency

1.2 PRE-ECLAMPSIA AND ECLAMPSIA

Pre-eclampsia Signs:
- High blood pressure (≥ 140/90 mmHg)
- Proteinuria (protein in urine)
- Edema (swelling, especially face and hands)
- Severe headache
- Epigastric pain (upper abdomen)
- Vision changes (blurred, spots, flashing lights)
- Reduced urine output

Eclampsia (Pre-eclampsia with Seizures):
- All above signs PLUS
- Convulsions/seizures
- Loss of consciousness
REFER URGENTLY (HIGH) - Life-threatening

Management:
- Monitor blood pressure regularly
- Test urine for protein
- Watch for danger signs
- REFER for specialist care

Medication (if available):
- Magnesium sulfate to prevent convulsions
- Antihypertensives if BP very high
- Hospitalization recommended

1.3 GESTATIONAL DIABETES

Risk Factors:
- Family history of diabetes
- Age > 35 years
- Obesity
- Previous gestational diabetes
- Polyhydramnios (excessive amniotic fluid)

Symptoms:
- Often asymptomatic
- Excessive thirst
- Frequent urination
- Unexplained weight loss

Screening:
- Blood glucose testing
- Usually 24-28 weeks

Management:
- Dietary modification
- Regular exercise
- Blood sugar monitoring
- Insulin if needed
- Regular fetal monitoring
- Close follow-up postpartum

Risks:
- Macrosomia (large baby)
- Birth trauma
- Neonatal hypoglycemia
- Increased risk of type 2 diabetes postpartum

1.4 PRETERM LABOR

Definition: Labor < 37 weeks gestation

Signs:
- Uterine contractions (regular, every 5-10 minutes)
- Vaginal bleeding
- Fluid leakage (rupture of membranes)
- Pelvic pressure
- Lower back pain
- Abdominal cramping

Risk Factors:
- Multiple pregnancy
- Infection
- Placental issues
- Incompetent cervix
- Trauma
- Polyhydramnios
- Previous preterm birth

REFER URGENTLY (HIGH):
- Any signs of preterm labor
- Fever with preterm labor signs
- Severe abdominal pain
- Rupture of membranes

Management:
- Hospitalization
- Fetal monitoring
- Antibiotics if appropriate
- Corticosteroids for fetal lung maturity
- Tocolytics (labor-stopping drugs) if appropriate
- Delivery timing depends on complications

SECTION 2: LABOR AND DELIVERY COMPLICATIONS
========================================

2.1 PROLONGED LABOR

Definition: Labor lasting > 12-18 hours without progress

Causes:
- Ineffective contractions (too weak, too infrequent)
- Cephalopelvic disproportion (baby too large, pelvis too small)
- Malpresentation (baby not head down)
- Maternal exhaustion
- Dehydration

Signs:
- Minimal cervical dilation progress
- Exhausted mother
- Swollen cervix
- Maternal fever (possible infection)
- Fetal distress signs

Complications:
- Maternal infection (chorioamnionitis)
- Uterine rupture
- Fetal hypoxia
- Fetal death
- Maternal hemorrhage

REFER URGENTLY (HIGH):
- Any prolonged labor
- With fever
- With fetal distress
- With maternal exhaustion

Management:
- Assess progress regularly
- IV fluids
- Nutrition support
- Pain management
- Augmentation (pitocin if available)
- Surgical intervention (C-section) if needed
- Antibiotics if infection

2.2 OBSTRUCTED LABOR

Causes:
- Small pelvis (contracted pelvis)
- Large baby
- Abnormal presentation
- Uterine rupture risk

Signs:
- No progress despite strong contractions
- Maternal distress
- Very strong, tetanic contractions
- Difficulty touching baby (high presenting part)
- Maternal fever
- Foul-smelling discharge
- Maternal shock signs
- Fetal distress

Complications:
- Uterine rupture (life-threatening)
- Maternal death if untreated
- Fetal death
- Maternal infection
- Fistula formation (long-term)

REFER URGENTLY (HIGH) - EMERGENCY:
- Suspected obstructed labor
- Signs of uterine rupture
- Signs of maternal shock

Management:
- EMERGENCY CESAREAN SECTION
- IV access
- Fluids
- Blood transfusion if available
- Antibiotics
- Urgent surgical care

2.3 ECLAMPSIA IN LABOR

Signs:
- Convulsions during labor
- High blood pressure
- Severe headache
- Vision changes
- Edema

REFER URGENTLY (HIGH) - EMERGENCY

Management:
- Magnesium sulfate IV
- Delivery (expedite)
- Monitor for complications
- Intensive care

2.4 SHOULDER DYSTOCIA

Definition: Baby's shoulder stuck behind mother's pubic bone after head delivery

Signs:
- Head delivers but body does not follow
- Retracting chin
- No progress

Risk Factors:
- Maternal diabetes
- Maternal obesity
- Large baby
- Prolonged labor

MANAGE IMMEDIATELY:
1. Do NOT pull on baby's head
2. Call for help
3. Suprapubic pressure
4. Gentle suprapubic pressure
5. McRoberts maneuver (sharp hip flexion)
6. Episiotomy if necessary
7. Internal maneuvers if needed
8. Prepare for emergency procedures

Refer URGENTLY (HIGH) if not resolved quickly

Risks:
- Brachial plexus injury (arm paralysis)
- Clavicle fracture
- Hypoxia from delay
- Fetal death if prolonged

SECTION 3: POSTPARTUM HEMORRHAGE
===============================

Definition: Blood loss > 500 mL within 24 hours of delivery

Causes:
- Uterine atony (floppy uterus - most common)
- Retained placenta or tissue
- Genital tract trauma
- Coagulopathy (bleeding disorder)
- Amniotic fluid embolism

Risk Factors:
- Prolonged labor
- Multiparity (many previous pregnancies)
- Multiple pregnancy
- Large baby
- Instrumented delivery
- Placenta previa/abruption

Early Warning Signs:
- Increased vaginal bleeding
- Pale appearance
- Rapid heart rate
- Low blood pressure
- Restlessness
- Decreased urine output
- Shock signs

REFER URGENTLY (HIGH) - EMERGENCY

Management - IMMEDIATE ACTIONS:
1. Keep mother in horizontal position
2. Elevate legs
3. IV access (large bore if possible)
4. Start IV fluids (500 mL rapidly)
5. Call for help
6. Assess bleeding source
7. Uterine massage (firm, circular motions)
8. Ensure bladder is empty (catheterization if needed)
9. Breastfeeding (stimulates uterine contraction)
10. Blood transfusion preparation

Medications (if available):
- Oxytocin (IV or IM) to cause uterine contraction
- Ergot alkaloids (IM) to cause uterine contraction
- Misoprostol (oral/rectal) to cause contraction
- Antibiotics if infection
- Blood products if severe

REFER IMMEDIATELY for:
- Uncontrolled bleeding
- Signs of shock
- Need for blood transfusion
- Need for surgical intervention

SECTION 4: POSTPARTUM INFECTION (PUERPERAL FEVER)
======================================

Definition: Temperature ≥ 38°C on any of first 10 days after delivery (excluding first 24 hours)

Causes:
- Endometritis (uterine infection) - most common
- Wound infection (episiotomy, C-section)
- Urinary tract infection
- Mastitis (breast infection)
- Septic thrombophlebitis (vein infection)

Risk Factors:
- Prolonged rupture of membranes
- Prolonged labor
- Instrumented delivery
- Cesarean section
- Retained products
- Poor hygiene

Signs:
- Fever
- Foul-smelling lochia (vaginal discharge)
- Abdominal pain or tenderness
- Chills
- Headache
- Malaise
- In severe: shock signs

REFER URGENTLY (MEDIUM to HIGH):
- Any postpartum fever
- Signs of severe infection
- Fever with systemic symptoms

Management:
- Hospitalization
- Blood cultures if possible
- Broad-spectrum antibiotics
- IV fluids
- Supportive care
- Monitor for sepsis
- Treatment of specific infection

Prevention:
- Clean delivery
- Proper hygiene
- Proper wound care
- Breastfeeding
- Early mobilization
- Adequate nutrition

SECTION 5: FAMILY PLANNING
==========================

Contraceptive Methods:

Barrier Methods:
- Condoms (male/female)
- Diaphragm
- Cervical cap
- Spermicides

Hormonal Methods:
- Oral contraceptive pills
- Injectable contraceptives
- Implants
- Patches
- Rings

Intrauterine Devices (IUDs):
- Copper IUD (non-hormonal)
- Hormonal IUD
- High effectiveness
- Can be inserted after delivery

Permanent Methods:
- Tubal ligation (female sterilization)
- Vasectomy (male sterilization)
- Both are permanent and effective

Natural Methods:
- Rhythm method
- Lactational amenorrhea
- Coitus interruptus
- Ovulation method

Selection Factors:
- Effectiveness
- Reversibility
- Side effects
- Cost
- Religious/cultural preferences
- Partner involvement
- Personal preference

Counseling Important For:
- Informed choice
- Realistic expectations
- Side effect management
- Continued use

SECTION 6: POSTPARTUM CARE
=========================

First 24-48 Hours:
- Monitor vital signs
- Check uterus involution
- Monitor bleeding
- Promote breastfeeding
- Pain management
- Nutrition and hydration
- Catheter care if applicable
- Wound care
- Psychological support

First 2 Weeks:
- Home visits if possible
- Assess for complications
- Breastfeeding support
- Nutrition counseling
- Exercise gradually
- Perineal care
- Mental health screening
- Family planning counseling

Postpartum Exercise:
- Start gradually (week 2-3)
- Pelvic floor exercises
- Abdominal strengthening
- Progressive aerobic activity
- Return to normal activity by 6 weeks

Warning Signs Requiring Medical Care:
- Excessive bleeding
- Fever
- Severe pain
- Signs of depression/psychosis
- Inability to care for self
- Difficulty bonding with baby

SECTION 7: NEWBORN SCREENING
============================

Screening Tests (if available):
- Blood tests for metabolic disorders
- Hemoglobin S screening (sickle cell)
- Bilirubin level (jaundice)
- Thyroid function

Hearing Screening:
- Early detection of deafness
- Intervention possible

Vision Screening:
- Check for cataracts
- Red reflex testing

Physical Examination:
- Complete pediatric exam
- Screen for congenital defects
- Developmental assessment

Growth and Development:
- Regular monitoring
- Vaccination updates
- Developmental milestones
