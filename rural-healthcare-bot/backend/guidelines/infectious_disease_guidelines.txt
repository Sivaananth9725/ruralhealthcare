INFECTIOUS DISEASE SURVEILLANCE AND CONTROL
============================================

SECTION 1: DISEASE SURVEILLANCE
==============================

Reportable Diseases (Routine):
- Tuberculosis
- HIV/AIDS
- Hepatitis
- Typhoid fever
- Dengue fever
- Malaria
- Diarrheal diseases
- Respiratory infections
- Sexually transmitted infections
- Vaccine-preventable diseases

Surveillance Functions:
1. Case identification
2. Case confirmation
3. Reporting
4. Data analysis
5. Response implementation
6. Follow-up
7. Evaluation

Outbreak Investigation:
- Define cases
- Count cases
- Calculate incidence/prevalence
- Identify common source
- Implement control measures
- Provide feedback to community

SECTION 2: TUBERCULOSIS (TB)
============================

Transmission:
- Airborne (through respiratory droplets)
- Prolonged close contact (> 8 hours)
- Usually active pulmonary TB

Risk Factors:
- HIV infection
- Malnutrition
- Diabetes
- Chronic kidney disease
- Immunosuppression
- Crowded living conditions
- Smoking
- Alcohol abuse

Symptoms (Pulmonary TB):
- Chronic cough (> 3 weeks)
- Hemoptysis (coughing blood)
- Chest pain
- Fever
- Night sweats
- Weight loss
- General weakness

Diagnosis:
- Sputum smear microscopy (if available)
- Chest X-ray
- GeneXpert MTB/RIF (if available)
- Clinical symptoms

Treatment:
- Standard: 6-month course
- Intensive phase: 2 months (Isoniazid, Rifampicin, Pyrazinamide, Ethambutol)
- Continuation phase: 4 months (Isoniazid, Rifampicin)
- Directly Observed Therapy (DOT) - staff witnesses each dose
- Adherence critical

Complications if Untreated:
- Disseminated TB
- TB meningitis
- TB of bones/joints
- Chronic lung damage
- Death

Prevention:
- BCG vaccination
- Infection control
- Nutritional support
- TB preventive therapy for HIV patients
- Smoking cessation

Contact Investigation:
- Identify close contacts
- Screen for TB
- Treat latent TB if positive

Refer to TB clinic/specialist

SECTION 3: HIV/AIDS
==================

Transmission:
- Sexual contact (unprotected)
- Blood-to-blood contact (shared needles)
- Mother-to-child (pregnancy, delivery, breastfeeding)
- Blood transfusion

Stages:
Stage 1: Acute infection
- Fever, rash, lymphadenopathy
- Often mistaken for flu

Stage 2: Asymptomatic (can last years)
- CD4 count declining
- Person is infectious

Stage 3: Symptomatic HIV
- CD4 count 200-500
- Opportunistic infections begin
- Symptoms depending on CD4 level

Stage 4: AIDS (CD4 < 200)
- Severe opportunistic infections
- Cryptococcal meningitis
- Tuberculosis
- Pneumocystis pneumonia
- Cytomegalovirus
- Toxoplasmosis
- Candidiasis

Diagnosis:
- Rapid antibody test
- Western blot confirmation
- CD4 count
- Viral load

Treatment (ART - Antiretroviral Therapy):
- Start when CD4 < 350 or diagnosed
- Usually triple therapy
- High adherence essential
- Regular monitoring
- Refer to HIV specialist

Prevention:
- Safe sex practices
- Condoms
- Testing of blood products
- Prevention of mother-to-child transmission
- Post-exposure prophylaxis (if exposed)

Support:
- Counseling
- Nutritional support
- Opportunistic infection prevention/treatment
- Psychosocial support
- Disclosure considerations
- Partner notification

SECTION 4: HEPATITIS
===================

Hepatitis A:
- Transmission: Fecal-oral (contaminated food/water)
- Symptoms: Fever, jaundice, abdominal pain, diarrhea
- Acute only, no chronic
- Prevention: Sanitation, vaccination, hand hygiene
- Recovery: Usually 4-6 weeks

Hepatitis B:
- Transmission: Blood, sexual contact, mother-to-child
- Can be chronic
- Prevention: Vaccination, safe practices
- Treatment: Antivirals if chronic
- Monitor for liver damage
- Screen contacts

Hepatitis C:
- Transmission: Bloodborne (needles, transfusion)
- Can be chronic
- Treatment: Antivirals (expensive)
- Screen for chronic infection

Hepatitis E:
- Transmission: Fecal-oral
- Usually self-limited
- Severe in pregnant women
- Prevention: Safe water, sanitation

Symptoms (General):
- Jaundice (yellowing of skin/eyes)
- Fatigue
- Abdominal pain
- Nausea/vomiting
- Dark urine
- Pale stools
- Fever (sometimes)

Diagnosis:
- Liver function tests
- Serologic tests
- Ultrasound

Management:
- Supportive care
- Monitor for complications
- Refer if severe
- Prevent transmission
- Vaccination (if available)

Complications:
- Fulminant hepatic failure (REFER URGENTLY)
- Cirrhosis (chronic)
- Hepatocellular carcinoma (chronic)

SECTION 5: SEXUALLY TRANSMITTED INFECTIONS (STIs)
==============================================

Common STIs:

Gonorrhea:
- Symptoms: Urethral discharge, dysuria, pelvic pain
- Complications: Pelvic inflammatory disease, infertility
- Treatment: Antibiotics (resistance developing)
- Screen: Urine culture

Chlamydia:
- Often asymptomatic
- Complications: PID, infertility, ectopic pregnancy
- Treatment: Antibiotics
- Screen: Urine tests, swabs

Syphilis:
- Stages: Primary (ulcer), secondary (rash), tertiary (late complications)
- Congenital: Affects fetus/newborn
- Diagnosis: Blood tests
- Treatment: Penicillin
- Early treatment prevents complications

Herpes Simplex:
- Recurrent painful vesicles/ulcers
- Treatment: Antivirals for symptoms
- Prevent transmission
- Cesarean if active at delivery

HPV (Human Papillomavirus):
- Often asymptomatic
- Can cause cervical cancer
- Prevention: Vaccination
- Diagnosis: Cervical smear
- Treatment: Ablation/excision

Trichomonas:
- Vaginal discharge, dysuria
- Treatment: Metronidazole
- Treat partners

Management of STIs:
1. Diagnosis confirmation
2. Partner notification and treatment
3. Appropriate antibiotics
4. Follow-up testing (cure verification)
5. Safe sex education
6. Complication screening
7. Psychological support

Prevention:
- Condoms
- Monogamy
- STI screening
- Partner treatment
- Sexual education
- Vaccination (HPV)

SECTION 6: DISEASE CONTROL AND PREVENTION
=========================================

Infection Control Measures:

Standard Precautions:
- Hand hygiene
- Personal protective equipment (PPE)
- Respiratory hygiene
- Safe injection practices
- Safe waste disposal
- Environmental cleaning

Airborne Precautions:
- Negative pressure room (if available)
- N95 respirators
- For: TB, measles, chickenpox

Droplet Precautions:
- Masks for close contact
- For: Flu, pertussis, meningitis

Contact Precautions:
- Gloves, gowns
- For: Diarrheal diseases, skin infections

Isolation:
- Separate from other patients
- Dedicated staff if possible
- Dedicated equipment
- Special ventilation

Quarantine:
- Expose individuals kept from community
- For: Potential disease exposure
- Typically 10-14 days

Outbreak Response:
1. Confirm outbreak exists
2. Investigate source
3. Implement immediate control measures
4. Identify and manage cases
5. Prevent transmission
6. Provide treatment
7. Communicate with community
8. Document and report
9. Evaluate response
10. Implement long-term prevention

SECTION 7: VECTOR-BORNE DISEASE CONTROL
======================================

Malaria Vector Control:
- Bed net distribution
- Indoor residual spraying
- Larval source management (drainage of breeding sites)
- Environmental management
- Community awareness
- Early diagnosis and treatment

Dengue Control:
- Water container management (remove standing water)
- Bed net use (daytime biting mosquitoes)
- Repellent use
- Protective clothing
- Community mobilization
- Early case detection

Tick-borne Disease Control:
- Protective clothing
- Inspect body after outdoor activity
- Proper removal of ticks
- Safe pesticide use (if needed)
- Animal management

Other Vectors:
- Sanitation improvements (diarrheal disease reduction)
- Waste management
- Animal control (rabies, plague)
- Dietary measures (food safety)

SECTION 8: FOOD AND WATER SAFETY
===============================

Water Safety:
- Source protection
- Treatment (boiling, chlorination, filtration)
- Safe storage
- Water quality monitoring
- Promotion of use

Boiling:
- 1 minute rolling boil adequate
- Higher altitudes: 3 minutes
- Inexpensive, reliable method
- Needs fuel

Chlorination:
- Household level (bleach 0.5-1.0 mL per liter)
- Monitor residual chlorine
- Good for stored water

Filtration:
- Sand filters
- Ceramic filters
- Cloth filters (basic)
- Combination approaches most effective

Food Safety:
- Clean: Wash hands, utensils, produce
- Separate: Raw and cooked foods
- Cook: Proper temperatures
- Chill: Refrigerate perishables
- Consume: Use before spoilage

Risk Foods:
- Raw meats
- Raw seafood (if from contaminated water)
- Unpasteurized dairy
- Raw produce from contaminated water
- Unrefrigerated prepared foods

Food Storage:
- Clean, dry containers
- Covered
- Proper temperature
- Away from pests
- Separate from toxins/chemicals

SECTION 9: HYGIENE AND SANITATION PROMOTION
==========================================

Community Education:
- Critical for disease prevention
- Focus on:
  - Handwashing
  - Toilet use
  - Water safety
  - Food hygiene
  - Waste management

Behavior Change:
- Identify barriers
- Address misconceptions
- Positive role models
- Community participation
- Incentives (sometimes)
- Repeated messaging

Key Messages:
1. Handwashing prevents disease
2. Latrines must be used
3. Drinking water must be clean
4. Food must be cooked well
5. Waste must be disposed properly
6. Vaccination protects children

Monitoring:
- Coverage rates
- Compliance
- Health outcomes
- Community feedback

SECTION 10: ANTIMICROBIAL RESISTANCE
===================================

Problem:
- Overuse of antibiotics
- Inappropriate use
- Incomplete treatment courses
- Use in animals
- Poor infection control
- Creates resistant organisms

Consequences:
- Treatment failures
- Prolonged illness
- Higher mortality
- Increased costs
- Untreatable infections

Prevention Strategies:
1. Antibiotic Stewardship:
   - Use only when necessary
   - Use appropriate agent
   - Use correct dose
   - Use correct duration
   - Avoid combination therapy unnecessarily

2. Infection Control:
   - Reduce transmission
   - Vaccination
   - Sanitation
   - Reduces antibiotic need

3. Surveillance:
   - Track resistant organisms
   - Guide treatment
   - Identify outbreaks

4. Regulation:
   - Prescription requirements
   - Ban over-the-counter sales
   - Control in agriculture
   - Limit veterinary use

5. Education:
   - Healthcare workers
   - Patients
   - Community
   - About appropriate use
   - About not sharing antibiotics

Clinical Practice:
- Culture before antibiotics when possible
- Choose narrow-spectrum when possible
- Use correct dose for correct duration
- Avoid unnecessary combinations
- Monitor response
- Switch to narrower spectrum if possible
- De-escalate when appropriate

Patient Education:
- Take complete course even if feeling better
- Do not share antibiotics
- Do not save leftover antibiotics
- Do not pressure for antibiotics for viral illnesses
- Proper use prevents resistance
